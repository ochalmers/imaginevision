## 1. The Category

### What Configurable Commerce Means

Configurable commerce is the commercial and technical infrastructure required to sell products that have options.

A sofa comes in 200 fabric choices. A mattress has firmness, size, and height permutations. A kitchen has units, finishes, appliances, and layouts. An appliance has colour, connectivity, and feature variants.

These products cannot be sold the same way commodities are sold. They require:
- Visual representation of every valid configuration
- Logic that enforces which configurations are possible
- Commerce mechanics that connect configuration to purchase
- Programmatic systems that manage this at scale across thousands of SKUs and variations

Most brands are solving this with workarounds — thousands of pre-rendered images, spreadsheet-managed options, fragmented tooling, manual asset creation pipelines. It is expensive, slow, and brittle.

Imagine is the platform infrastructure that makes configurable commerce work properly.

### Why This Is a Category, Not a Feature

"Visualisation" describes a capability. "Rendering" describes a technical method. "Image generation" describes an output format.

None of these describe the problem that brands actually have.

The problem is: *how do we sell products that have options, at scale, in a way that works commercially?*

The answer to that problem is a category — configurable commerce — and the platform that enables it. Positioning Imagine as a visualisation tool undersells the commercial scope of what is being built and misaligns Imagine with the buyers who have budget authority.

A CMO does not have budget for "rendering." They have budget for solving configurable commerce.

### The Category Playbook

Category creation requires:
1. **Naming the problem** before naming the solution
2. **Educating the market** about why the old approach is broken
3. **Defining the new standard** so competitors are measured against it
4. **Building the infrastructure** that makes the category real

The website is the primary vehicle for executing steps 1–3. Step 4 is the product.

## 2. Positioning

### Positioning Statement

**For brands that sell configurable products, Imagine is the platform infrastructure that powers their configurable commerce — enabling them to visualise, configure, and sell any product at scale, without the cost and fragility of traditional approaches.**

### What Imagine Is Not

Clarity requires exclusion. The website must not position Imagine as:

- A **rendering tool** — this frames it as a creative production service
- A **CGI replacement** — this anchors it to a workflow problem, not a commerce problem
- An **image generation platform** — this conflates it with generative AI tools
- A **product configurator** — this is one output of the platform, not the platform itself
- A **3D viewer** — this describes a UI component, not an infrastructure layer

### Competitive Frame

Imagine should not be compared to visualisation vendors. The competitive frame is:
- vs. **custom-built internal tooling** — "You've built this yourself. It's expensive to maintain and impossible to scale."
- vs. **fragmented point solutions** — "You're using five different tools for what should be one platform."
- vs. **doing nothing / workarounds** — "You're photographing 400 SKUs per product update. There's a better way."

The aspiration frame (who Imagine wants to be compared to):
- **Stripe** — for making complex commercial infrastructure simple and programmable
- **Vercel** — for giving developers a platform that removes infrastructure complexity
- **Contentful** — for making product content structured and API-first

### Messaging Hierarchy

**Level 1 — Category statement (homepage hero)**
> Imagine powers configurable commerce.

**Level 2 — What this means (homepage sub)**
> The platform infrastructure for brands that sell products with options.

**Level 3 — How (platform/features)**
> Render any configuration. Enforce product logic. Power interactive experiences. Build on a programmable API.

**Level 4 — Why it matters (proof)**
> Reduce asset production costs. Increase product page conversion. Launch new variants in hours, not weeks.

## 3. Audience Strategy

### Audience 1: Companies (Commercial Buyers)

**Who they are**
Brands and retailers that sell configurable physical products. This includes:
- Furniture brands (DTC and wholesale)
- Mattress manufacturers and retailers
- Kitchen and bathroom brands
- Appliance companies
- Lighting brands
- Any brand with significant product variation

**What they need from the website**
- Confidence that Imagine understands their commercial problem (not just the technical one)
- Evidence that it works at scale with real brands
- A clear sense of what implementation looks like
- Commercial proof: ROI, cost reduction, time to market
- A path to a conversation with a commercial team

**Job titles making decisions**
- Chief Marketing Officer / VP Marketing
- Chief Digital Officer / VP Digital
- Head of E-commerce / Digital Commerce
- Head of Product Marketing
- VP Product (for product experience decisions)
- IT/Technology Director (for platform evaluation)

**Their questions**
- "Will this actually work with our existing commerce stack?"
- "How long does it take to get live?"
- "What does the asset pipeline look like?"
- "Can we see it working for a brand like ours?"
- "What does it cost?"

**Website journey for companies**
Homepage → Solutions (by use case) → Industries (by vertical) → Case Studies → Request Demo

### Audience 2: People (Technical Builders)

**Who they are**
Developers, designers, product managers, and agencies building product experiences — either for Imagine customers or as the technical implementers within those organisations.

**What they need from the website**
- Technical credibility — the API and SDK need to feel well-designed and well-documented
- Quick time-to-understanding — can they evaluate the platform in under 20 minutes?
- Code they can actually run — examples, sandbox, quickstart
- Confidence that Imagine is built for developers, not just sold to them

**Job titles**
- Frontend Engineer / Full-stack Developer
- Technical Lead / Engineering Manager
- Solution Architect
- Digital Designer / Creative Technologist
- Agency Technical Lead

**Their questions**
- "What does the API look like?"
- "How does it integrate with our frontend framework?"
- "Is there a React/Vue/vanilla JS SDK?"
- "What's the rate limiting and pricing model?"
- "Can I see a working demo?"

**Website journey for people**
Homepage → Developers hub → API docs → Examples → SDK → Get API Key / Start Building

### Audience Separation Strategy

The two audiences have different mental models, different questions, and different conversion actions. The website must serve both without confusing either.

**Navigation split:**
- "Solutions" and "Industries" tabs speak to companies
- "Developers" tab speaks to people
- "Platform" speaks to both (technical buyers and developers)

**Language split:**
- Company-facing language: "configure", "visualise", "launch", "convert", "scale"
- Developer-facing language: "API", "SDK", "integrate", "programmatic", "render pipeline"

**CTA split:**
- Companies: "Request a demo" / "Talk to us"
- Developers: "Get API access" / "Read the docs" / "View examples"

## 4. Content Strategy

### Content Pillars

**Pillar 1: Category Education**
Content that names and defines configurable commerce. Guides, explainers, and frameworks that help buyers understand why their current approach is broken and what the right model looks like.

Key content types:
- "What is configurable commerce?" (foundational guide)
- "The true cost of manual asset production"
- "Why product configurators fail" (and what to do instead)
- Industry state-of-play reports

**Pillar 2: Product Depth**
Technical and product content that demonstrates platform capability for both buyers and builders.

Key content types:
- Platform deep-dives (rendering pipeline, configuration engine, API design)
- Technical tutorials
- Integration guides
- Changelog / product updates

**Pillar 3: Commercial Proof**
Evidence that Imagine works at scale with real brands.

Key content types:
- Case studies (with commercial outcomes: conversion lift, cost reduction, time to market)
- Customer spotlights
- Before/after comparisons
- ROI frameworks

**Pillar 4: Developer Community**
Content that builds credibility and relationship with the technical audience.

Key content types:
- Open-source contributions and examples
- API design notes
- SDK changelogs
- Developer tutorials and quickstarts

### Content Principles

1. **Show, don't just tell** — every product claim should be demonstrated, not just stated
2. **Commercial outcomes over technical features** — lead with what changes for the business, then explain how
3. **Specificity earns trust** — "40% reduction in asset production costs" beats "reduce costs"
4. **Developer content is product** — treat the docs and examples as part of the product experience, not an afterthought
5. **Category before company** — content that builds the category builds Imagine's authority faster than content that only promotes Imagine

## 5. Conversion Strategy

### Two Conversion Paths

**Enterprise / Commercial Path (Companies)**

Goal: Get a qualified demo request from a brand buyer.

Funnel:
1. Awareness — category content, organic search, events, PR
2. Consideration — solutions pages, industry pages, case studies
3. Evaluation — demo request form, sales conversation
4. Decision — proposal, proof of concept, contract

Key conversion actions:
- Primary CTA: "Request a demo"
- Secondary CTA: "See how it works" (video or interactive demo)
- Tertiary CTA: "Download guide" (lead capture for education stage)

**Developer / PLG Path (People)**

Goal: Get an active API key / developer in the product.

Funnel:
1. Awareness — developer content, GitHub, technical communities
2. Exploration — developers hub, API docs, examples
3. Activation — free API key, quickstart guide
4. Expansion — usage growth, team adoption, upsell to commercial plan

Key conversion actions:
- Primary CTA: "Get API access" / "Start building"
- Secondary CTA: "View examples" / "Read the docs"
- Tertiary CTA: "Join developer community"

### CTA Architecture

The website must never ask both audiences to do the same thing. Page-level CTA strategy:

| Page | Company CTA | Developer CTA |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Request a demo | Get API access |
| /platform | Request a demo | Read the docs |
| /solutions/* | Request a demo | See example code |
| /industries/* | Talk to us | — |
| /developers | — | Get API key |
| /developers/docs | — | View quickstart |
| /case-studies | Request a demo | — |
| /about | Talk to us | Join us (careers) |

### Trust Architecture

Trust must be earned before conversion is attempted. The following elements should be distributed throughout the site:

**For companies:**
- Named customer logos (even if case study is not yet published)
- Outcome statistics ("40% faster", "60% cost reduction" — with attribution)
- Named integrations (Shopify, Salesforce Commerce Cloud, SAP, etc.)
- Recognisable brand names in "works with" or "trusted by"

**For developers:**
- Code samples on marketing pages (not just in docs)
- API response examples that look well-designed
- GitHub repository links
- Open-source components
- Clear API versioning signals

## 6. How the Website Supports Platform Growth

### Phase 1 — Category Definition (Launch)
Website establishes the category, defines the problem, introduces Imagine as the platform owner.

Metrics: Brand awareness, demo request volume, developer sign-up rate, content engagement.

### Phase 2 — Vertical Depth (3–6 months post-launch)
Industries section expands with full vertical pages. Case studies published. Industry-specific landing pages for campaigns.

Metrics: Vertical-specific demo requests, case study engagement, organic search growth by vertical keyword.

### Phase 3 — Platform Expansion (6–12 months)
New platform capabilities get their own pages. Developer ecosystem grows. Partner directory launches.

Metrics: API key activations, developer community size, partner revenue, inbound from developer referrals.

### Phase 4 — Community and Ecosystem
Blog and guides become a genuine resource for the configurable commerce category. Imagine starts owning the category in search and conversation.

Metrics: Organic traffic, newsletter subscribers, community members, conference/event presence.

## 7. Design and Tone Principles

### Design References

- **Stripe** — clean layouts with technical credibility; code blocks as first-class UI elements; conversion architecture built into every page
- **Vercel** — dark, performance-forward aesthetic; developer-first without excluding non-technical buyers
- **Linear** — opinionated, minimal product design; confidence in what is included and excluded
- **Apple** — product storytelling at the headline level; visuals that do the arguing

### Tone of Voice

**Clear** — say one thing per sentence. No hedging. No filler.
**Strategic** — frame problems before solutions. Give buyers a mental model.
**Technical but accessible** — developers should feel at home; executives should not feel lost.
**Premium** — the language and design should feel like a category leader, not a startup scrambling for positioning.
**Confident** — Imagine is building the category. The tone should reflect that conviction.

### What to Avoid

- **Feature lists without context** — "Our platform includes X, Y, Z" without explaining why it matters
- **Passive voice** — "products can be configured" vs. "brands configure any product"
- **Startup jargon** — "unlock," "leverage," "empower," "seamless," "game-changing"
- **Over-claiming** — specificity beats superlatives
- **Two audiences in one sentence** — pick who you're talking to

## 8. Measurement Framework

### Business Metrics
- Demo requests per month (companies)
- Developer API key activations per month
- Cost per qualified lead from organic vs. paid

### Website Metrics
- Homepage → demo conversion rate
- Homepage → developer sign-up conversion rate
- Average session depth by audience segment
- Case study engagement rate
- Developer docs return visitor rate

### Content Metrics
- Organic search ranking for category keywords ("configurable commerce", "product configurator platform", "product visualisation API")
- Content-attributed pipeline
- Guide download rate (email capture)
- Blog → demo conversion path attribution

### Leading Indicators (early signal)
- Direct navigation to /developers (developer awareness)
- Case study scroll depth (buyer seriousness)
- API docs time on page (developer evaluation quality)
- Contact form qualification (right buyer profile)
